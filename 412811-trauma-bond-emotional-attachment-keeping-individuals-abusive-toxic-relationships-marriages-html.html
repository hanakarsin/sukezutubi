Trauma Bond: Experts Say Emotional Attachment Keeps Individuals in Toxic Relationships and Marriages

Publish date: 2024-03-13
  • Despite rampant cases of abuses in relationships and marriages, some parties choose to stay despite the risk they are exposing themselves to
  • Psychologists have attributed this behaviour to an emotional attachment known as trauma bonding
  • A recent video of a man slapping a woman after an argument and demanding that she leaves has ignited the conversation around trauma bonding

Have you ever asked yourself why there are those close friends who go through abusive relationships and marriages but rather choose to stick to their unions instead of walking away?

Others have received numerous apologies from their oppressors and chosen to reunite, a move that has not always worked in their favour at the end of it.

A recent video showing a Kenyan man beating a woman and ordering her to leave the place they were having a good time at ignited discussions online after the woman seemed unmoved and chose to stay.

This is just one among many women and men currently in abusive relationships in the country.

They bear physical and emotional scars of assault, and despite the regular and habitual aspects of abuse, they hardly see the danger it will all culminate into.

TUKO.co.ke reached out to psychologists to try and understand the reasons behind this trend.

The professional psychologists observed that this attachment, even in situations where one is abused, is referred to as trauma bonding.

“It is a situation where an individual’s feelings are subjected to trial. In most scenarios, abuse cases usually begin with love and affection before they suddenly turn to the opposite,” explained Serah Muindi, a Counseling Psychologist at Eldoret-based Hopewell Firm.

The victims usually struggle to understand how people who excited their emotions suddenly turn to hurt their feelings.

What is trauma bonding?

“Most people in relationships tend to be taken by surprise when the person they loved turns against them. The worst part is when the offender apologises and the matter is settled making the victim regain trust. Sadly, once someone has been abused, the process continues,” added Muindi.

This cycle usually cements a bond between the hurt individual and the inflictor of pain.

“Trauma bonding often makes it possible for the abused person to form a strong attachment with the abuser to the extent of feeling sympathy for them despite going through difficult times,” she described the cycle.

According to Muindi, the fear of the unknown leads many people to sink into trauma bonding without their knowledge.

“An individual who has been abused by those close to them will immediately begin to have fear of the unfamiliar. They often ask themselves how life will be without their partners or oppressors around. Others insist they are well familiar with those who abuse them thus able to tolerate the acts,” she noted.

The counsellor said some individuals think twice about the repercussions of leaving the abusive relationships and fears they develop they choose to stick by with hopes of changing the person’s character in their favour.

What causes abusive marriages in Kenya

“At times those subjected to abusive marriages will always put a line of defence by protecting the oppressor. They would make assumptions that if they love their aggressors harder and forgive them more they would become nice. This is what makes the victims remain in unsafe situations. The oppressors on the other hand take advantage of this situation and hurt them more,” Muindi indicated.

Caroline Okanda, another psychologist, opined that lack of social support system, poverty, and lack of collateral are some of the contributors to trauma bonding.

“Similarly, lack of insight due to unavailability of counsellors or someone to share with them is also partly to blame because they hardly know they are in a problem hence don’t know way out,” Okanda said.

She observed that if the trauma is countered, it will be possible to break the bond between the oppressor and the victim.

Okanda further noted that the victim must avoid self-blame over situations they are in, arguing that such a move would continue keeping one in the toxic relationship.

“It will also be wise to talk to someone and get professional assistance on how to overcome the issue,” she added.
